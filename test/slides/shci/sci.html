Working Smarter, Not Harder

Ecorr. = EFCI - EHF


  • A small fraction of configurations are responsible for the majority of the correlation energy
  • We only want to treat the important configurations exactly
  • How do we know which configurations are important (without solving the full problem)?
  • Selected CI: iteratively grow a set of important configurations and treat the remainder approximately (usually with PT)
  • It turns out in an FCI wave function, that most of the configurations aren't important. Only a small fraction of the configurations are responsible for the majority of the correlation energy.
  • Which means that we're spending a lot of "precious" computational resources on configurations that aren't that important.
  • But the problem is: how can we know which configurations are the important ones without solving the full problem?
  • Enter the "selected" CI methods, which iteratively grow a set of important configurations and treat the remainder perturbatively.
  • Our work focuses on a particular flavor of these methods called Heat-bath CI (HCI), where we grow this set efficiently.
  • In practice, this means that we can correlate many more electrons than FCI.
  • FCI/CASCI is limited to correlating around 22e in 22o, we have treated systems with almost two hundred orbitals.
  • Finally, a bit of jargon that I need to introduce. In HCI we use a single parameter \( \epsilon_1 \). Just like in experiments we have little knobs to adjust the settings and \( \epsilon_1 \) is our little knob.
  • We can continuously tune all the way from HF to FCI, which a nice feature, and throughout the rest of the talk, we will show convergence to the FCI answer.
